Menu Sports New Team Water Land Paralympics Games Videos Blog CBC Kids blog 10 big moments in Olympic Games history (Aris Messinis/AFP via Getty Images) You probably know that the Olympic Games are an international sports competition that happens every two years. But do you know when, how and why they started? Do you know when Canada got its first Olympic Games or its first medals? Let's take a look at some big moments in the history of the Olympic Games. 776 BC — the first-ever Olympics Dancers dressed as priestesses hand over the Olympic flame during the ceremonial lighting of the Olympic flame in Ancient Olympia, Greece, Thursday, April 21, 2016. (AP Photo/Thanassis Stavrakis) OLYMPIA, GREECE — The first-ever Olympics were held in Greece on the plains of Olympia — that's where the name "Olympics" comes from! Women couldn't compete in these Olympic Games. 1896 — the first modern Olympic Games Runners getting ready to run the 100-metre course at the Games of the I Olympiad in Athens, Greece, 1896. (public domain) ATHENS, GREECE — The first modern Olympic Games included just nine sports. Winners didn’t receive three levels of medals like they do today, instead, winners received an olive branch, silver medal and diploma and second place received a copper medal, laurel branch and diploma. 1904 — Canada sends its first team /> Winnipeg Shamrocks lacrosse team, Olympic gold medal champions at the St. Louis Games of 1904. (Wikimedia/public domain). ST. LOUIS, US — These were the first Olympics to have three level of medals: gold, silver and bronze. Canada sent it's first team to the Olympic Games, a lacrosse team that was all Mohawk nation players. 1908 — the longest Olympic Games German skaters Anna Hübler and Heinrich Burger, 1908. (Wikimedia/public domain). LONDON, UK — The 1908 Olympic Games were originally going to be held in Rome, but then Mt. Vesuvius erupted so everything had to be moved. They lasted six months and four days — the longest Olympics ever! Oddly enough, figure skating was included since the Games went on until October. The closing ceremony was held on Halloween. Spooky! 1936 — the Olympic Games come to TV American runner Jesse Owens breaks the record in the 200 metre race and wins gold at the 1936 Olympic Games in Berlin, Germany. (Library of Congress Prints and Photographs Division Washington, D.C. 20540/public domain) BERLIN, GERMANY — 1936 was the year the Olympics came to television — but only locally. The big story of the Games was American runner Jesse Owens who won four gold medals. These were also the first Olympic Games to see the torch relay, where the lighted torch was carried from Olympia in Greece to the site of the Games. 1976 — Canada gets the Olympic Games Montreal’s Olympic Stadium (Flickr by abdallah licensed CC BY 2.0) MONTREAL, QUE. — The Olympic Games are held in the summer for the first time in Canada in locations around Montreal, Quebec. Many of the buildings are still in use today. 2012 — women from every country compete Wojdan Shaherkani, the first female competitor from Saudi Arabia, at the London 2012 Olympic Games. (Flickr by Martin Hesketh licensed CC BY 2.0) LONDON, UK — For the first time in history, women competed from every country. Saudi Arabia had its first female athletes, one of them a 16-year-old named Wojdan Shaherkani, who competed in judo. 2014 — women athletes get to fly Austria's Daniela Iraschko-Stolz attends the 1st jump at the women's ski jumping normal hill individual training, at the Sochi 2014 Olympic Games. (Getty Images) SOCHI, RUSSIA — The men's ski jumping event is full of thrills and chills. But up until 2014, women could not participate in the sport. Women's ski jumping finally debuted in Sochi. 2016 — refugee athletes get their own team Members of the Refugee Olympic Team pose in front of the Christ the Redeemer statue in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, 2016. (Buda Mendes/Gett Images) RIO DE JANEIRO, BRAZIL — For the first time in Olympic Games history, a team of refugee athletes competed under the Olympic flag. The 10 athletes came from South Sudan, Ethiopia, DRC and Syria. 2018 — Africa's first bobsledders Seun Adigun and Akuoma Meoga of Nigeria slide during the women's bobsleigh, at the PyeongChang 2018 Olympic Games. (Clive Mason/Getty Images) PYEONGCHANG, SOUTH KOREA — Even though they were the second Nigerian participants at an Olympic Games in the winter, the women's Nigerian bobsled team became Africa's first Olympic bobsledders.   More Fun!
